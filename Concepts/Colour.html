Colour ​

Colour is a property of light

It is the way our eyes perceive the different wavelengths of light. The colour of an object is determined by the wavelengths of light that it reflects. For example, a red apple appears red because it reflects red light and absorbs other colours. The colour of an object can also be affected by the way light interacts with its surface, such as refraction, diffraction, or interference

There are three primary colours:

  • Red
  • Green
  • Blue

By combining these colours in different ways, we can create all the other colours of the spectrum. This is how colour is produced in television and computer screens, as well as in printing and painting

Our eyes have three types of colour receptors, with each type sensitive to a different range of wavelengths. These receptors work together to create the full spectrum of colours that we can see. The way our eyes perceive colour is influenced by factors such as the intensity of the light, the angle of the light, and the background against which the colour is viewed

Our eyes are more sensitive to some colours than others. For example, we are more sensitive to green light than red or blue light

  • Camera sensors are more sensitive to green light (50% green, 25% red, 25% blue)

Colour Space ​

It is a mathematical model that represents the way our eyes perceive colour

RYB Colour Space ​

The RYB colour space is based on the subtractive colour model, which states that the primary colours of pigments are red, yellow, and blue. By combining these colours in different ways, we can create all the other colours of the spectrum. This is how colour is produced in painting and printing

  • R: represents the amount of red pigment
  • Y: represents the amount of yellow pigment
  • B: represents the amount of blue pigment

Variations:

  • CMY: Cyan, Magenta, Yellow
  • CMYK: Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Black

RGB Colour Space ​

The RGB colour space is based on the additive colour model, which states that the primary colours of light are red, green, and blue. By combining these colours in different intensities, we can create all the other colours of the spectrum. This is how colour is produced in television and computer screens

  • R: represents the amount of red light
  • G: represents the amount of green light
  • B: represents the amount of blue light

Variations:

  • sRGB: Standard RGB (1996) - Web
  • Linear RGB (2010) - HDR
  • A98 RGB: Adobe RGB (1998) - Printing

XYZ Cromaticity Diagram ​

The XYZ diagram is a colour space that was created by the International Commission on Illumination (CIE) in 1931. It is based on the trichromatic theory of colour vision, which states that the human eye has three types of colour receptors: red, green, and blue. These receptors are sensitive to different wavelengths of light, and they work together to create the full spectrum of colours that we can see

  • X: represents the amount of red light
  • Y: represents the amount of green light
  • Z: represents the amount of blue light

Limitations of the XYZ diagram:

  • It is not intuitive
  • It does not correspond to the way we perceive colour
  • It does not take into account the brightness of the colours

LUV Colour Space ​

The CIELUV colour space was developed by the CIE in 1976 as an improvement on the XYZ diagram. It is based on the opponent-process theory of colour vision, which states that the human eye has three pairs of colour receptors: red-green, blue-yellow, and black-white. These receptors work together to create the full spectrum of colours that we can see

  • L: represents the lightness of the colour
  • U: represents the red-green axis
  • V: represents the blue-yellow axis

The CIELUV colour space is more intuitive than the XYZ diagram, and it takes into account the brightness of the colours. It is widely used in the printing and graphic design industries

Usage:

  • Light design
  • TV and monitor calibration

Limitations of the CIELUV colour space:

  • It is not perceptually uniform
  • It does not take into account the way light interacts with objects

LAB Colour Space ​

The CIELAB colour space was developed by the CIE in 1976 as an improvement on the CIELUV colour space. It is based on the opponent-process theory of colour vision, and it is designed to be perceptually uniform, meaning that the distance between two colours in the colour space corresponds to the difference in their perceived colour

  • L: represents the lightness of the colour
  • A: represents the red-green axis
  • B: represents the blue-yellow axis

Usage:

  • Photography
  • Printing, Textiles
  • Graphic design

Variations:

  • OKLAB: Optimized LAB
  • Jzazbz: Perceptually uniform
  • ITP (ICtCp): Good luminance predictability

LCH Colour Space ​

The LCH colour space is a cylindrical representation of the CIELAB colour space. It is based on the opponent-process theory of colour vision, and it is designed to be perceptually uniform. The LCH colour space is more intuitive than the CIELAB colour space, and it is widely used in the printing and graphic design industries

  • L: represents the lightness of the colour
  • C: represents the chroma of the colour
  • H: represents the hue of the colour

Variations:

  • OKLCH: Optimized LCH
  • LCHuv: Perceptually uniform

HSL Colour Space ​

The HSL colour space is a cylindrical representation of the RGB colour space. It is based on the opponent-process theory of colour vision, and it is designed to be perceptually uniform. The HSL colour space is more intuitive than the RGB colour space, and it is widely used in the printing and graphic design industries

  • H: represents the hue of the colour
  • S: represents the saturation of the colour
  • L: represents the lightness of the colour

HSLuv is a variation of the HSL colour space that is designed to be perceptually uniform. It is based on the opponent-process theory of colour vision, and it is widely used in the printing and graphic design industries

The Colour Wheel ​

The colour wheel is a visual representation of how colours relate to each other. It is made up of three primary colours, three secondary colours, and six tertiary colours. The primary colours are red, blue, and yellow. The secondary colours are green, orange, and purple, which are created by mixing the primary colours. The tertiary colours are a combination of a primary and a secondary colour, such as red-orange or blue-green

  • Charles Hayter (1816)
  • Goethe and Schiller (1810)
  • Michel-Eugène Chevreul (1839)

Colour Harmony ​

Colour harmony is the way that colours interact with each other in a composition. There are several ways to achieve colour harmony, such as using complementary colours, analogous colours, or monochromatic colours. Complementary colours are colours that are opposite each other on the colour wheel, such as red and green. Analogous colours are colours that are next to each other on the colour wheel, such as blue and purple. Monochromatic colours are different shades of the same colour, such as light blue and dark blue

Colour Temperature ​

Colour temperature is a way of describing the colour of light. It is measured in degrees Kelvin (K), with lower temperatures representing warmer colours and higher temperatures representing cooler colours. For example, a candle flame has a colour temperature of around 1800K, while a clear blue sky has a colour temperature of around 10000K. Colour temperature is important in photography, film, and lighting design, as it can affect the mood and atmosphere of a scene

White balance is the process of adjusting the colour temperature of an image to make it appear more natural. This is done by setting the white balance of the camera to match the colour temperature of the light source. For example, if you are shooting under fluorescent lighting, you would set the white balance of the camera to match the colour temperature of the fluorescent lights

Colour Correction ​

Colour correction is the process of adjusting the colours in an image to make them appear more natural. This can involve adjusting the white balance, saturation, contrast, and brightness of the image. Colour correction is often used in photography, film, and graphic design to enhance the appearance of an image

Luminescence ​

Luminescence is the brightness of a colour. It is determined by the intensity of the light that is reflected by an object. Bright colours have a high luminescence, while dark colours have a low luminescence. Luminescence is an important factor in the way that colours interact with each other in a composition

Gamma Correction ​

Gamma correction is the process of adjusting the brightness of an image to make it appear more natural. It is used to compensate for the way that the human eye perceives light. Gamma correction is often used in photography, film, and computer graphics to enhance the appearance of an image

  • Gamma Curve: y = x^(1/gamma) where gamma is the gamma value, x is the input value, and y is the output value
  • 2.2 gamma is the standard for sRGB colour space

Colour Psychology ​

Colour psychology is the study of how colours affect human behaviour and emotions. Different colours can evoke different feelings and responses in people. For example, red is often associated with passion and energy, while blue is associated with calm and tranquillity. Colour psychology is used in marketing, design, and branding to create specific emotional responses in consumers

Terms ​

  • Hue: The colour of an object

    • It is determined by the wavelength of light that is reflected by the object
    • It is the most basic property of colour
    • It is what we typically think of as the colour of an object
    • It is the position of a colour on the colour wheel

  • Saturation: The intensity of a colour

    • It is determined by the purity of the colour, with more saturated colours being more intense
    • It is also known as chroma or colourfulness
    • The amount of grey in a colour
    • At maximum saturation, the colour is pure and vivid (no grey)
    • At minimum saturation, the colour is dull and muted (grey)
    • It is the distance of a colour from the grey axis

  • Brightness: The lightness of a colour

    • It is determined by the amount of light that is reflected by an object
    • It is also known as value or lightness
    • How much black is in a colour
    • At maximum brightness, the colour is light (no black)
    • At minimum brightness, the colour is dark
    • It is the position of a colour on the lightness axis

  • Tint: A colour that has been lightened by adding white

    • It is a mixture of a colour with white
    • It is a lighter version of the original colour
    • It is used to create pastel colours

  • Shade: A colour that has been darkened by adding black

    • It is a mixture of a colour with black
    • It is a darker version of the original colour
    • It is used to create deep colours

  • Tone: A colour that has been muted by adding grey

    • It is a mixture of a colour with grey
    • It is a less intense version of the original colour
    • It is used to create neutral colours

Display Technologies ​

Pixel ​

A pixel is the smallest unit of a digital image. It is a single point in a raster image, and it is made up of a combination of red, green, and blue subpixels. By varying the intensity of these subpixels, we can create all the other colours of the spectrum. Pixels are used in television and computer screens, as well as in printing and photography

Colour Depth ​

Colour depth is the number of bits used to represent the colour of a pixel. It determines the number of colours that can be displayed in an image. The higher the colour depth, the more colours that can be displayed. For example, an 8-bit colour depth can display 256 colours, while a 24-bit colour depth can display over 16 million colours

  • 1 subpixel = 1-bit/channel (0 or 1)

  • 1 pixel = 3 subpixels (RGB) = 3-bits (RGB) - 8 colours

    • 1-bit/channel (3-bits in total) can represent 8 colours

Delta E (ΔE) ​

Delta E (ΔE) is a metric that is used to measure the difference between two colours. It is used in the printing and graphic design industries to ensure that the colours in an image are accurate. The lower the ΔE value, the more similar the colours are. A ΔE value of less than 1 is considered to be imperceptible to the human eye

  • ΔE < 2 is considered good colour accuracy

Colour Gamut ​

Colour gamut is the range of colours that can be displayed by a device. It is determined by the colour space and colour depth of the device. The wider the colour gamut, the more colours that can be displayed. Colour gamut is an important factor in the way that colours are displayed in television and computer screens, as well as in printing and photography

  • RGB
  • sRGB: Standard RGB (1996) - Web
  • Adobe RGB (1998) - Printing
  • DCI-P3: Digital Cinema Initiatives - Film
  • Display P3: Apple's version of DCI-P3
  • Rec. 2020: Ultra High Definition (UHD) - TV
  • Rec. 2100: High Dynamic Range (HDR) - TV
  • ProPhoto RGB: Wide Gamut - Printing

Wide Gamut:

  • Supports about 75% of the visible colours

High Definition (HD) ​

High Definition (HD) is a standard for high-quality video and audio. It is defined by the resolution of the video, which is typically 1920x1080 pixels. HD video has a higher resolution and better quality than standard definition video. HD is used in television, film, and video games to provide a more immersive viewing experience

  • Rec. 709: Standard HD colour space

  • Supports about 35% of the visible colours

  • Image Resolution: 1920x1080 pixels

  • Frame Rate:

    • 24/P, 24/PsF, 23.976/P, 23.976/PsF (for motion pictures)
    • 50/P, 25/P, 25/PsF, 50/I (for regions with 50 Hz systems like PAL/SECAM)
    • 60/P, 59.94/P, 30/P, 30/PsF, 29.97/P, 29.97/PsF, 60/I, 59.94/I (for regions using 60 Hz systems like NTSC)

  • Primary Chromaticities:

    • White Point: D65 (0.3127, 0.3290)
    • Red: (0.640, 0.330)
    • Green: (0.300, 0.600)
    • Blue: (0.150, 0.060)

Colour Profile ​

A colour profile is a set of data that describes the colour characteristics of a device. It includes information about the colour space, colour gamut, and colour depth of the device. Colour profiles are used in the printing and graphic design industries to ensure that the colours in an image are accurate. They are also used in television and computer screens to calibrate the display

Types of Displays ​

  1. CRT (Cathode Ray Tube): Older technology, bulky, heavy, low resolution

    • Very high refresh rate
    • High contrast ratio
    • High colour accuracy
    • High power consumption

  2. LCD (Liquid Crystal Display): Thin, lightweight, high resolution

    • Low contrast ratio
    • Low colour accuracy
    • Low power consumption
    • Slow response time

  3. LED (Light Emitting Diode): Bright, energy-efficient, high resolution

    • Good contrast ratio
    • Good colour accuracy
    • Low power consumption
    • Low pixel density

  4. OLED (Organic Light Emitting Diode): Thin, flexible, high contrast

    • Very high contrast ratio
    • Very high colour accuracy
    • Low power consumption
    • High pixel density

  5. AMOLED (Active Matrix Organic Light Emitting Diode): Bright, energy-efficient, high contrast

    • Very high contrast ratio
    • Very high colour accuracy
    • Low power consumption
    • High pixel density

  6. QLED (Quantum Dot Light Emitting Diode): Bright, energy-efficient, high contrast

    • Very high contrast ratio
    • Very high colour accuracy
    • Low power consumption
    • High pixel density

  7. QD-OLED (Quantum Dot Organic Light Emitting Diode): Bright, energy-efficient, high contrast

    • Very high contrast ratio
    • Very high colour accuracy
    • Low power consumption
    • High pixel density

  8. MicroLED: Bright, energy-efficient, high contrast

    • Very high contrast ratio
    • Very high colour accuracy
    • Low power consumption
    • High pixel density
